Data Privacy
KNL-DAT-001 · Last reviewed April 2026

Data privacy is about treating people's personal information with respect. "Personal data" means any information that can identify a real person — their name, email, phone number, location, health details, financial information, and more. Handling this data carelessly can harm real people and expose KNEURALABS to serious legal consequences.

Individual rights — what people can ask us to do

  • Right to access: "Show me what data you hold about me." We must respond within 30 days with a copy of their data.
  • Right to correction: "Fix this wrong information about me." We must update it promptly.
  • Right to deletion: "Delete all data you hold about me." We must comply unless we have a legal reason to keep it (e.g., an ongoing contract or legal dispute).
  • Right to object: "Stop using my data for marketing." We must stop immediately.
  • If anyone sends you a data rights request, forward it to the legal team the same day. The 30-day clock starts immediately.

Data breaches — what to do if data is lost or exposed

  • A data breach is any incident where personal data is accessed, shared, or lost without authorisation.
  • Examples: You email a client file to the wrong person. A laptop containing client data is stolen. A hacker gains access to our system.
  • Report immediately: Tell your manager and the IT security team as soon as you discover or suspect a breach. Do not wait to investigate it yourself first.
  • Leadership must be informed within 2 hours.
  • In many cases, we are legally required to notify regulators (like data protection authorities) within 72 hours. Missing this deadline can result in large fines.
  • Do not attempt to cover up or minimise a breach. Early, honest reporting always leads to better outcomes.

Sharing data internationally

  • Personal data collected in Europe (under GDPR) cannot be sent to countries outside Europe unless special safeguards are in place.
  • The main safeguard is called Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) — legal agreements approved by European regulators.
  • If your project involves transferring European personal data to the US, Asia, or anywhere outside the EU, check with the legal team before proceeding.
  • This is not bureaucracy for its own sake — violations can result in fines of up to 4% of global annual revenue under GDPR.

Practical tips for everyday privacy

  • Do not cc unnecessary people on emails containing personal data. Send to the minimum number of recipients needed.
  • Use "Bcc" (blind carbon copy) when emailing a list of people who do not know each other — so they cannot see each other's email addresses.
  • Do not discuss client personal data in public places, on calls in shared spaces, or over unsecured chat apps.
  • If you receive personal data that you were not expecting or do not need, tell the sender and delete it.

Do

  • Collect only the personal data you truly need.
  • Forward data rights requests to legal the same day.
  • Report a suspected data breach within 2 hours.
  • Use Bcc when emailing groups who don't know each other.
  • Securely delete data when it's no longer needed.

Don't

  • Collect personal data "just in case it's useful."
  • Email client files to the wrong person — double-check.
  • Cover up or downplay a data breach.
  • Transfer EU data abroad without legal sign-off.
  • Discuss personal data in public or on unsecured chat apps.
For vendors handling personal data on our behalf: Sign a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) before accessing any personal data. The DPA defines what data you can use, how, and for how long. Operating without one is a serious violation.
