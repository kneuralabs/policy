Legal & AI Rules
KNL-LEG-001 · Last reviewed April 2026

AI is a new technology and governments around the world are creating new laws to regulate it. This section explains the key laws that affect our work and what they mean for you in practice. You do not need a law degree to understand these — we have written them in plain English.

The EU AI Act — Europe's AI law

The European Union has passed a law that classifies AI systems into four risk levels:

  • Unacceptable risk (banned): AI that manipulates people's behaviour without their knowledge, AI used for mass surveillance, AI that scores citizens like a credit score for social control. We do not build or use these.
  • High risk (extra checks required): AI used in hiring, medical decisions, credit scoring, or law enforcement. If we or our clients use AI in these areas, extra safety checks and documentation are required before deployment.
  • Limited risk: Chatbots and AI that interact with people. Users must be told they are talking to an AI, not a human.
  • Minimal risk: Spam filters, AI in video games, etc. These have few restrictions.
  • What this means for you: Before building or deploying any AI system for a client, ask our legal team which risk category it falls into.

NIST AI Risk Management Framework — our internal process

NIST is a US government body that publishes best practices. We follow their four-step AI framework:

  • Govern: Set clear policies and roles for AI use (this handbook is part of this step).
  • Map: Identify the risks of each AI system we build or use before we start.
  • Measure: Test AI systems regularly to check they are performing as expected and not causing harm.
  • Manage: Fix problems when found. Keep records. Update systems when laws or risks change.

Privacy laws — GDPR (Europe) and CTDPA (Connecticut, USA)

  • These laws give individuals the right to control their personal data.
  • Right of access: Anyone can ask to see what personal data we hold about them.
  • Right to deletion: Anyone can ask us to delete their data ("right to be forgotten").
  • Right to correction: Anyone can ask us to fix incorrect data about them.
  • We must respond to all such requests within 30 days. If you receive such a request, forward it to our legal team immediately — the 30-day clock starts the moment we receive it.

Contracts — always have a signed agreement before starting work

  • Never start any work for a client — not even a small task — without a signed written agreement.
  • Verbal agreements are not contracts. Emails that say "yes, go ahead" are not contracts.
  • Contracts must be reviewed and signed by the CEO or MD.
  • Work you create belongs to KNEURALABS: Any code, document, design, or idea you create while working for us is our intellectual property — not yours. This applies even if you created it at home outside of work hours, as long as it relates to your work.

AI-generated content and copyright

  • Be careful using AI-generated text, images, or code in client deliverables. Copyright law around AI output is still evolving.
  • Always disclose to the legal team if a deliverable contains significant AI-generated content.
  • Do not represent AI output as your original human-created work to clients without disclosure.
For legal vendors and partners: Must be licensed to practice in Connecticut, actively track emerging AI regulations, provide quarterly compliance reports, and carry professional liability insurance.
